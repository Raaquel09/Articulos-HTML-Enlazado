ABSTRACT
Provenance plays a major role when understanding and re-
using the methods applied in a scientic experiment, as it
provides a record of inputs, the processes carried out and
the use and generation of intermediate and nal results. In
the specic case of in-silico scientic experiments, a large
variety of scientic workow systems (e.g., Wings, Taverna,
Galaxy, Vistrails) have been created to support scientists.
All of these systems produce some sort of provenance about
the executions of the workows that encode scientic ex-
periments. However, provenance is normally recorded at a
very low level of detail, which complicates the understanding
of what happened during execution. In this paper we pro-
pose an approach to automatically obtain abstractions from
low-level provenance data by nding common workow frag-
ments on workow execution provenance and relating them
to templates. We have tested our approach with a dataset
of workows published by the Wings workow system. Our
results show that by using these kinds of abstractions we
can highlight the most common abstract methods used in
the executions of a repository, relating dierent runs and
workow templates with each other.

2.1 Terminology
A workow template connects the steps of the workow
together, its inputs, intermediate results and expected out-
puts, and denes their types and dependencies. There are
two types of workow templates, as illustrated in Figure 1:
1. Abstract workow template: Template where some steps
of the workow are not bound to a specic component
(i.e., a particular implementation of an algorithm, web
service, etc.). The abstract workow template aims to
capture the scientic method used in an experiment,
independently from a specic tool or service. For ex-
ample, Figure 1 shows an abstract template on the
left, where the Stemmer and TermWeighting com-
ponents are methods that can be specialized with dif-
ferent algorithms shown in the component taxonomy
of the right of the gure.
2. Specialized workow template: Template in which all
the steps are bound to a specic service, tool or code.
It is more specic than the abstract workow tem-
plate. In Figure 1, two specialized workow templates
are shown in the middle: every step specializes a step
of the same abstract template (left) according to the
taxonomy of components (on the right of the gure).
A workow execution provenance trace is a structured log
of the workow execution results. It contains details on
how every intermediate result and output was generated by
each of the steps and their dependencies (i.e., the complete
provenance of the execution).
We assume for this work that both workow templates
and execution provenance traces can be represented as di-
rected acyclic graphs (DAGs), which is the most common
representation in data-oriented scientic workows. There-
fore templates with conditionals and loops are out of the
scope of this paper.
2.2 Types of workow abstractions
Catalogs of patterns (or of problem solving methods) rel-
evant for the creation of complex methods have been exten-
sively studied in the literature for a large number of scientic
and non-scientic domains [25, 8, 22]. Given a set of work-
ow templates and/or provenance execution traces we aim
to detect the following two types of patterns:
1. Internal Macros: This kind of abstraction refers to
groups of steps in a workow that correspond to repet-
itive patterns of combined tasks. An example can be
seen in Figure 2, showing a workow for document
classication. Part of the branches of the workow per-
form the same tasks (StopWords, SmallWords, Stem-
mer, TF IDF, Multi2Single, FormatAr), so we can
consider them as an internal macro. Although this
pattern might be easy to spot manually in simpler tem-
plates, it gets increasingly hard to detect when work-
ows grow in size.
2. Composite workows: Abstraction referring to a work-
ow composed by one or more sub-workows. In this

3. AUTOMATIC DETECTION AND GENER-
ALIZATION OF WORKFLOW ABSTRAC-
TIONS
Given a dataset that contains both workow templates
and workow execution provenance traces, our goal is to
detect Internal Macros and Composite Workows. We face
two major challenges in our work:
1. Detection of common workow fragments in the work-
ow dataset. It is important to note that we are not
interested in retrieving just the largest common sub-
workows, but also the most frequent smaller work-
ows.
2. Generalization of workow fragments to derive abstract
workow templates. For instance, two dierent work-
ow templates may be in fact specializations of the
same abstract workow template. By noting explicitly
their common abstract workow fragments, we make
them comparable and easier to understand (both are
alternative implementations of the same method).
We describe next how we have approached each of these
of challenges.
3.1 Common workow fragment detection
If we consider a workow as a dataow graph, we can
use graph algorithms to detect common fragments as com-
mon subgraphs. We represent the workow templates and
the workow execution provenance traces as labeled graphs.
Nodes are labeled with the types of the workow compo-
nents of the taxonomy (e.g., TF or LovinsStemmer in Fig-
ure 1) and the types of the data being used and produced
in the workow (e.g., Dataset in Figure 1); while the edges
are labeled with the dependencies of the workow (usage
when a computational process consumes an input and gener-
ation when a computational step produces an output). Our
goal is nding the maximum overlapping subgraphs between
N dierent graphs, where N corresponds to the number of
workows or execution provenance traces available in the
dataset. Since this problem can be reduced to the subgraph
isomorphism problem between two graphs, its complexity is
NP-Complete [7].
Trying to make a naive comparison of workows in a
repository (e.g., by comparing them in an all-against-all
manner) would lead to inecient and limited solutions for
the problem that we are trying to solve, given its complexity.
To address this, we use the SUBDUE algorithm [16], which
allows learning the most relevant context-free grammar from
a set of labeled graphs. This grammar highlights the most
frequent structures found in the graph and evaluates each
candidate structure according to the way it compresses the
overall collection graph. SUBDUE proposes to use two dif-
ferent metrics for compressing the graph with the production
rules of the grammar:
1. The Minimum Description Length (MDL), where the
best structure is the one that minimizes the description
length of the entire data set (i.e., the bits needed for
its encoding) [6].
2. The size of the graph, which aims at nding the struc-
ture that best reduces the size of the overall collection
graph.
We apply SUBDUE independently for each of the work-
ows to capture Internal Macros, and taking as input the
whole collection to detect Composite Workows. A ltering
step is then applied to the results of SUBDUE in order to
remove the non complex and repeated structures.

3.2 Workow template and workow execu-
tion provenance trace generalization
A key feature of our approach is that we can general-
ize workow templates and workow execution provenance
traces in order to derive abstract workow fragments. The
set of abstract workow fragments is based on a taxonomy
of components that can be associated to a catalog of work-
ow components. An example of this type of abstraction
can be seen in Figure 1, where starting from any of the spe-
cialized templates in the center plus the taxonomy shown
at the right of the gure we can generalize the specialized
templates to the abstract template on the left.
This generalization requires an additional step of the in-
put graphs before executing the SUBDUE algorithm. The
generalization step replaces the types of the nodes of a given

with their appropriate superclass in the taxonomy of com-

multi-step fragments (i.e., fragments that combine at least

a processing step). The number of ltered multi-step frag-

smaller fragments with the same number of occurrences as
the larger fragment) is highlighted in Table 1 because it rep-
resents the nal workow fragments found.
Our goal is to maximize the number of ltered multi-step
fragments, since regular multi-step fragments may be dis-
carded. For example, Figure 2 shows a multi-step fragment
composed of six steps (StopWords, SmallWords, Stemmer,
TF IDF, Multi2Single and FormatAr) and also a smaller
multi-step fragment with just SmallWords and StopWords
(included in the larger fragment). The ltering step discards
the smaller fragment and keeps the larger one because they
appear with the same frequency in the workow (twice). If
the workow had a third branch with a StopWords step and
a SmallWords step, the number of occurrences of the frag-
ment would be three instead of two and therefore it would
be considered a ltered multi-step fragment.
The total number of multi-step fragments is higher than
the ltered multi-step fragments due to the way the algo-
rithm operates. SUBDUE takes the input graph and ex-
pands each of the nodes in every iteration, returning the
best fragment compressing the graph according to the met-
rics described in Section 3.1. Therefore the most general
fragment is returned in the latest iterations of the algorithm,
using the fragments detected previously. In contrast, in the
manual annotation only the most general fragment is con-
sidered.
The best results are obtained when running the algorithm
to nd multi-step fragments and ltering them. These re-
sults match almost perfectly the ones found with the manual
analysis. Most of the multi-step fragments of the manual
analysis have been found with the application of our ap-
proach (2 out of 3 ltered multi-step fragments in the work-
ow execution provenance traces, 3 out of 3 ltered multi-
step fragments in the workow templates). The number of
internal macros detected in the workow execution prove-
nance traces is lower because some executions had failed,
and hence the provenance graph corresponding to that part
of the workow was missing in the execution provenance
traces.
Another interesting fact is the high number of fragments
that are discarded from those obtained by SUBDUE (i.e.,
not ltered multi-step fragments), with a maximum of 31
out of 39 in executions with generalization. These fragments
usually contain a single processing step, so they are not rel-
evant for our results. Their presence is motivated in the
executions by the instantiation of collections. An example
can be seen in Figure 2, where the Multi2Single step pro-
duces a collection of Single1 results that are consumed in
parallel by a collection of FormatAr steps. In templates,
the number of occurrences of discarded fragments is high
when a single component is shared among many templates
(with dierent specializations of the same abstract method).

Manual and automatic analyses show dierent results be-

Although most of the results obtained by the algorithm
share the main core of common workow fragments, the best
results are found in the workow execution provenance traces
with generalization and using the size metric (up to 10 multi-

The explanation of this result is related to the number of ex-
ecutions that specialize abstract workow templates in dif-
ferent ways: when applying generalization, the fragments
found are likely to be grouped around the abstract template
with most executions. In contrast, generalization does not
improve signicantly the number of multi-step fragments in
templates, as the amount of specialized templates available
in the dataset is not high (in most cases just the abstract
templates are available). Similarly to what happened in the
Internal Macro analysis, the results of the executions dier
from those obtained by the templates because of collections
and incomplete executions. Therefore, the workow frag-
ments detected in the executions represent the most com-
mon portions of workows executed successfully.

5. RELATED WORK
Finding commonalities among fragments of workows is
related to the workow discovery and graph-based compar-
isons problems. Goderis and colleagues [12] apply sub-graph
isomorphism techniques combined with semantic technolo-
gies for creating a ranking to nd the similarities between a
repository of workow and an input workow. Similarly, in
[1], Bergmann and Gil use graph matching techniques and
semantic annotations to nd similarities between a template
and a repository of existing workows. Our work is dierent
from these approaches in that we are not interested on the
relation of singular templates with other templates of the
repository, but common fragments among all of them.
Other work approaches this problem from data mining or
case-based reasoning perspectives. Leake and Kendall-Mor-
wick [17] propose to use case-based reasoning over prove-
nance execution traces in order to help users with sugges-
tions for the creation of new workows. The suggestions
are created detecting similarities of the new workow with
previous existing ones, which is another form of sub-graph
isomorphism. Yaman et al [26] also propose to mine ex-
isting provenance executions, combining the control ow
and dataow of existing workows in order to approximate
9
http://www.oeg-upm.net/files/dgarijo/kcap2013Eval
to a target workow. This work is integrated within the
POIROT framework [4], used to learn complex hierarchical
task models from user-generated traces. The main dierence
with respect to our work is that we aim at making explicit
the relation among the workows in the dataset rather than
learn or suggest similar workows to a given one. However,
while our purpose is not to learn the main method derived
from the workow dataset, the workow fragments that we
detect could be suggested as new templates for users.
Finally, another area of related work is the automatic de-
tection of Problem Solving Methods (PSMs), which describe
the reasoning process to achieve the goal of a task in an
implementation and domain-independent manner. Gomez-
Perez and Corcho [14] used graph matching techniques to
nd specic PSMs in provenance logs. This is related to our
work since we also look for fragments in a workow dataset,
but in our case the fragments are not dened beforehand, as
it is the case for that work. These common fragments could
be seen as automatically detected PSMs, since they encode
the common method shared among several workows for
achieving a subgoal.
6. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
In this paper we have described an approach for the au-
tomatic detection of the most common workow fragments
among a scientic workow dataset, using as input a repos-
itory of workows that can be either workow templates or
workow execution provenance traces. We have focused on
two types of workow fragments: those that occur within
a workow (Internal Macros) and those that occur among
several workows (Composite Workows). We have shown
that our proposed approach is able to detect ltered com-
plex fragments successfully and generalize fragments from
workows and provenance traces of their executions.
Our approach uses SUBDUE algorithm, which derives the
most relevant context-free grammar of a given graph. Our
results demonstrate that this approach nds the workow
fragments with most occurrences, improving the results of
manual sub-workow detection within the fragments of the
dataset.
We are currently pursuing dierent lines of work in or-
der to improve our approach. First, we are analyzing how
to detect other relevant abstractions regarding the relations
between workows, like workow overloading (i.e., detec-
tion of workows with the same processing steps but dif-
ferent datatypes). Second, we are looking to extend our
approach by adding extra data preparation steps to sim-
plify the graph, or even transformations [3] for reducing the
search space. Some of these improvements could help to
overcome the overlapping limitation exposed in Section 4.
We are also planning to test our approach with traces and
templates from other systems with existing taxonomies of
components, such as GenePattern [23] or Galaxy [10]; and
from systems without them, such as Taverna [20] or Vis-
trails [5]. Finally, we plan to explore workows with ad-
ditional constructs such as conditionals and iterations. We
have been using SUBDUE for DAGs, but the algorithm is
capable of discovering any fragment as long as it can be
encoded in a labeled graph (including cyclic graphs).
