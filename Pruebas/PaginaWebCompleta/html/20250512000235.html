AbstractThe landscape of workow systems for scientic
applications is notoriously convoluted with hund reds of seemingly
equivalent workow systems, many isolated research claims, and
a steep learning curve. To address some of these challenges and
lay the groundwork for transforming workows research and
development, the WorkowsRI and ExaWorks projects partnered
to bring the international workows community together. This
paper reports on discussions and ndings from two virtual
Workows Community Summits (January and April, 2021).
The overarching goals of these workshops were to develop a view
of the state of the art, identif y crucial research challenges in the
workows community, articulate a vision for potential community
efforts, and discuss technical approaches for realizing this vision.
To this end, participants identied six broad themes: FAIR com-
putational workows; AI workows; exascale challenges; APIs,
interoperability, reuse, and standards; training and education;
and building a workows community. We summarize discussions
and recommendations for each of these themes.
Index TermsScientic workows, community roadmap, data
management, AI workows, exascale comput ing, interoperability
I. INTRODUCTION
Scientic workow systems are used almost universally
across scientic domains for solving complex and large-
scale computing and data analysis problems, and have un-
derpinned some of the most signicant discoveries of the
past decades [1]. Many o f these workows have signicant
computational, storage, and communication demands, and thus
must execute on a wide range of large-scale platforms, fr om
local clusters over science or public clouds to upcoming
exascale HPC platforms [2]. Managing these executions is
often a signicant u ndertaking, requ iring a sophisticated and
versatile software infrastructure.
Historically, many of these infrastructures for workow
execution consisted of complex, integrated systems, developed
in-house by work ow practitioners with strong de pendencies
on a range of legacy tech nologieseven including sets of
ad-hoc scripts. D ue to the increasing need to support work-
ows, de dicated workow systems were developed to provide
abstractions for creating, executing, and adapting workows
conveniently and e fciently while ensuring portability. While
these efforts are all worthwhile individually, there ar e now
hundreds of independent workow systems [3]. These are
created and u sed by thousands of researchers and developers,
leading to a rapidly growing corpus of workows research
publications. The resulting workow system technology land-
scape is fragmented, w hich may present signicant barriers
for future workow users due to the tens of seemingly com-
parable, yet u sually mutually incompatible, systems that exist.
In the current workow research, there are conicting theo-
retical b a ses and abstractions for what constitutes a workow
system. It may be possible to translate between system s that
use the same underlying abstractions; however, the contrary is
not feasible. Specically, typical systems have a layered model
that abstractly underlies it: (i) if the models are the same for
two systems, they are compatible to some extent, and if they
implement the same layer s, they can be interchanged (mod ulo
some translation effort); (ii) if the models are the same for
two systems, but they are implemented by components a t
different layers, they can be co mplementary, and may have
common ele ments tha t could be shared; (iii) if the models
are distinct, workows or system components are likely not
exchangeab le or interoperable. As a result, many teams still
elect to build their own custom solutions r ather than adopt,

II. FAIR COMPUTATIONAL WORKFLOWS
The FAIR princip le s [9] have laid a foundation for sharing
and publishing digital assets and, in particular, da ta . The
FAIR principles emphasize machine a ccessibility and that all
digital assets should be Findable, Accessible, Interoperable,
and Reusable. Workows encode the methods by w hich the
scientic process is conducted and via which data are created.
It is thus important that workows both support the creation
of FAIR data and themselves adhere to the FAIR p rinciples.
A. Brief State-of-the-art and Challenges
Workows are hybrid processual digital assets that can be
considered as data or software, or some combination of both.
As such, there is a range of considerations to take into account
with respect to the FAIR pr inciples [10]. Some perspectives
are already well explored in data/software FAIRness, such as
descriptive metadata , software metrics, and versioning; how-
ever, workows create unique ch a llenges such as representing
a complex lifecycle from specication to execution via a
workow system , throu gh to the data created at the completion
of the workow execution.
As a specialized kind of software, workows have two prop-
erties that FAIRness funda mentally must address: abstraction
and composition. As far as possible a workow specication,
as a graph or some declarative expression, is abstracted from
its execution undertaken by a dedicated software platform.
Workows are composed o f modular building blocks and
expected to be remixed. FAIR applies all the way down
at the specication and execution level, and for the whole
workow and each of its components. One of the most
challengin g aspects of making workows FAIR is ensuring
that they can be reused. These challenges include being able to
capture and then move workow components, dependencies,
and application environments in such a way as not to affect the
resulting execution of the workow. Further work is required
to und erstand u se cases for reuse, before exploring methods
for capturing necessary context and enabling reuse in the same
or different environments.

B. A Vision for Potential Community Activities
Given current efforts for developing FAIR data and soft-
ware, it is importan t to rst understand what efforts could be
adapted to workow pr oblems. An immediate activity include
participating in working groups focused on applying FAIR
principles to data and so ftware. For instanc e , FAIR4RS [13]
coordinates community-led discussions aroun d FAIR princi-
ples for research software. Workows c ould then be initially
tackled fro m the point of view of workows as software,
which could originate a novel task force. Proposed working
groups such as FAIR for Virtua l Research Environments [14]
represent adequate progress towards this goal.
A fundamental tenet of FAIR is the universal availability
of machine processable metadata. The European EOSC-Life
Workow Collab oratory, for example, has developed a meta-
data fram ework for FAIR workows based on schema.org [15],
RO-Crate [16], and CWL [17]. This could be a community
starting point for standardization of metadata ab out workows.
An integral aspect of a FAIR computational workows task
force would be to collect a set of real-world use cases and
workows in several dom ains to examin e fr om the perspec-
tive of the FAIR data principles. This exercise will likely
highlight areas in which the FAIR data principles adequately
represent challen ges in workows. Based on these experiences,
a set of simple rules could be dened for creating FAIR
workows, similar to the ones in [18]. From these rules,
promin ent workow repositories (e.g., WorkowHub.eu [19]
and Dockstore [20]), communities, and workow systems
can dene recommendations to suppor t the development and
sharing of FAIR workows. These efforts relate not only
to the workows themselves, but the workow components,
execution environments, and the different types of data.
Ensuring provenance can capture the necessary information
is key for enabling FAIRness in workows. Many provenance
models [21] can be implemented or extended to capture the
informa tion needed for FAIR workows. Additionally, FAIR
principles are more likely to be followed if the process for
capturing these metrics is automated and embedded in work-
ow systems. In this case, a workow execution will become
FAIR by default, or per haps with minimal user curation.
III. AI WORKFLO WS
Articial in telligence (AI) and machine lear ning (ML)
techniques are becoming more and more p opular within the
scientic community. Workows increasingly integrate M L
models to guide analysis, couple simulation and data anal-
ysis codes, and exploit sp ecialized computing ha rdware (e.g.,
GPUs, neu romorphic chips) [22]. These workows inherently
couple various ty pes of tasks such as short ML inference,
multi-node simulations, long-running ML model training, etc.
They are also often iterative and dynamic, with lear ning
systems deciding in real time how to modify the workow,
e.g. by ad ding new simulations or cha nging the workow
all together. AI-enabled workow systems therefore must be
capable of optimally placing and managing sin gle- and multi-
node tasks, exp loit heterogeneous architectures (CPUs, GPUs,
and accelerators), and seamlessly coordinate the dynamic
coupling of disparate simulation tools.
A. Brief State-of-the-art and Challenges
Workows empowered with ML techniques largely differ
from traditional workows running on HPC machines. While
workows (i. e., one large-scale application simulating a scien-
tic object or process) traditionally take little inp ut data and
produce large outputs, ML approaches target model training,
which usually requires the input of a large quantity of data
(either via les or from a collection of databases) and produces
a small number of trained models. After training, these models
are used to infer new quantities (during model inference)
and behave like very lightweight applications th a t produce
small quantities of output data. These models can be stand-
alone applications, or even embe dded within larger traditional
simulations. There exists an inherent tension between tradi-
tional HPC, which evolved around executing large capacity-
style codes and AI -HPC, which requires the coordinated
execution of many smaller capability-scale a pplications (e.g.,
large ensembles of data generatio n co-mingled with inference
and coinciding with periodic retraining of models).
With its relian c e of data, effective AI-workows should pro-
vide ne-grained data management and versioning features,
as well as adequate data provenance capabilities. This da ta
management will have to be exible: some applications and
workows might need to m ove data via a le-sy stem, while
others could be be tter served from a tra ditional database, data
store, or a streaming dataow model. During inference, it may
be best to couple the (lightweight) model as close to the
data it is processing as po ssible. In any case, effective data
management is a key feature of successful AI workows.
Another key feature of AI workows is the inherent incor-
poration of non-traditional hardware, such as G PUs and tensor
processing units (TPUs), which can signicantly accelerate
both training and infer ence steps. Workow systems thus
need to pr ovide mechanisms for managing heterogeneous
resources o foading heavy computations to GPUs, and
managing data between GPU and CPU memory hierarchies.
Furthermore, since ML training and inference may be best
executed on different hardware from the main simulation,
AI workows might need to be executed on multi-machine
federated systems (with the main code executed on a tradi-
tional HPC system, but the ML model tra ining or inference
on a separate system). Additiona lly, it is also necessary to
provide tight integration to widely- used ML frameworks, the
development of which is not driven by the HPC community.
ML frameworks use Python and R-based libraries an d do
not follow the classic HPC model: C/C++/Fortran, MPI, an d
OpenMP, a nd submission to an HPC batch scheduler. Yet,
some efforts in the HPC community seem promising, e.g.
LBANN [23], EMEWS [24], [25], and eFlows4HPC [26].
Other approaches like Merlin [27] b le nd HPC and cloud
technologies to enable federated work ows. However, there
is a cle a r disconnect between HPC m otivations, needs, and
requirements, and AI/ML current practices.
Finally, one of the major differences between traditional and
AI workows is the inherent itera tive nature of ML processes
AI workows often feature feedback loops over a data set.
Data are created, the model is retr ained, and its accuracy
evaluated. ML training tasks might leverage hyperparameter
optimization fram eworks [28][30] to adjust their execution
settings in real time. The nal trained model is often used
to select new data to acquire (in an a ctive learning envi-
ronment, the model is used to decide which new simulations
to run to better train the mo del on the next iteration). By
design, ML-empowered workows are dynamic, in contrast to
traditional workows with mor e structured and deterministic
computations. At runtime, the workow execution graph can
potentially evolve based on internal metr ic s (accuracy), which
may reshape the graph or trigger task preemption. Workow
systems should thus support dynamic branching (e.g., condi-
tionals, criteria) and partial workow re-execution on-demand.
B. A Vision for Potential Community Activities
To address the disconnect between HPC systems an d prac-
tices and AI workows, the com munity needs to develop sets
of example use cases for sample problems with representative
workow structures and data types. In addition to exp anding
upon the above challenges, the community could codify
these challenges in example use cases. However, the set
of challenges for enabling AI workows is extensive. The
community thus ne eds to dene a systematic process for
identifyin g and categorizing these challenges. A short-te rm
recommendation would be to write a community white paper
about AI Workow challenges/needs.
Building from the use cases above for the needs and
requirements of AI workows, the c ommunity could dene
AI-Workow mini-apps, w hich could be used to pair with
vendors/future HPC developers so that the systems can be
benchm arked against these workows, and therefore support
the co-design of emerging o r future systems (e.g., MLCom-
mons [31] and the Collective Knowledge framework [32]).
IV. EXASCALE CHALLENGES AND BEYOND
Given the computational demand s of many work ows, it
is crucial that their execution be not only feasible but also
effortless and efcient on la rge-scale HPC systems, and in par-
ticular upcoming exascale systems [2]. Exascale systems are
likely to contain millions of independent computing elements
that ca n be concur rently scheduled across more than 10,0 00
nodes, millions of cores, and tens of thousands of acc e le rators.
A. Brief State-of-the-art and Challenges
HPC resource allocation policies and sched ulers designs
typically do not conside r workow applications: they provide a
mere job abstraction instead of workow-aware abstractions.
Workow u sers/systems are forced to make their workows
run on top of this ill-tted abstraction e. g., it is difcult
to control low-level behavior c ritical to workows (i.e., pre-
cise mapp ing of tasks to spe cic compute re sources on a
compute node). Furthermore, the re is a clear lack of support
for elasticity ( i.e., scaling up/down the number of nodes).
Overall, it is curren tly difcult to run work ows efciently and
conveniently on HPC systems without exten ding (or even over-
hauling) resource management/scheduling appro aches, which
ideally would allow programmable, ne-grain application-
level resource allocation and scheduling.
Related to the above challeng e, it is currently not possible
to support both workow and non-workow users harmo-
niously and/or efciently on the same system. So me features
needed by workows are often unavailable. For instance, batch
schedulers can support elastic jobs (e.g., Slurm); however,
experience shows that system admins may not be keen on
enabling this capability, as they deem long static allocations
preferable. A cultural change is perhaps needed as it seems
that workows are not yet con sid ered as high-priority appli-
cations by high-end compute facilities.
Hybrid architectures are key to high performance and
many workows can or a re specically designed to explo it
them. However, on HPC systems, the necessary resource
descriptions and mechanisms are not necessarily available to
workow users/systems (even though som e workow systems
have successfully interfaced to such mechanisms on particular
systems) [33]. Although these resource descriptions and mech-
anisms are typic a lly available as part of the job abstraction,
it is often not clear how a workow system can discover and
use them effectively.
Finally, fault-tolerance and fault-recovery have been ex-
tensively studied on exascale systems, with several works and
working solutions for tra ditional parallel jobs [34]. In the
context of scientic workows, specic techniques have been
the subject of several studies [35], however workow-specic
solutions are ty pically not readily available or deployed.
Moreover, workows are built on smaller platforms, thus
operating an d testing at exascale would entail expressing new
requirements/capabilities and dealing with new constraints
(e.g., what is a local exascale workow?).
B. A Vision for Potential Community Activities
An immediate activity consists in developing documen-
tation in the form of workow templates/recipes/miniapps
for execution on high-end HPC system s to be hosted on a
community web site. Some efforts u nderway provide partial
solutions [36]. For instance, collections of workows exist
but typically do not provide large scale execution capabilities
(e.g., community testbeds). Some compute facilities provide
workow tool documentation a nd help with their users [ 37].
These solutions should be cataloged as a starting point, and
HPC facilities could promote yearly workow days, in
which they give workow user s and developers training and
early access to machines to try out th eir workows, thus
gathering feedback from users and developers.
To drive the design of workow-aware abstractions, the
community could specify community benchmark workows
for exascale execution, exerting all relevant hardware as well
as functionality c apabilities. Then it becomes possible for
different workow systems to execute these benchmark s ini-
tial e fforts could build on previous benchmark solutions [38],
[39]. These benchmarks c ould then be included in exascale
machines acceptability tests. Note that th e re will be a ne e d
to pick particu lar workow systems to run these benchmarks,
which will foster training and education of HPC person nel.
Last, including workow requirements very early on in
machine procurement process for machines at computing
facilities will signicantly lower the barriers for enabling
workow execution and therefore porting workow applica-
tions. This effort is therefore preconditioned on the availabil-
ity of miniapps and/or ben chmark specications, as well as
API/scheduler specications, as outlined above.
V. APIS, INTEROPERABILITY, REUSE, AND STANDARDS
There has been an exp losion of work ow technologies in
the last decade [ 3]. Individual workow systems often serve
a particular user community, a specic underlying compute
infrastructure, a ded ic a te d software engineering vision, or
follow a specic historical trait. As a result, there are sub-
stantial technical and conceptual overlaps. Reasons for diver-
gence in clude (i) use cases require different workow struc-
tures, (ii) organizations have very different optimization goals,
(iii) predened execution systems provide fundamentally dif-
ferent capabilities, or (iv) availability and scarcity of different
types of r esources. Another reason is that it is relatively easy
to start building a workow system for a specic narrow
focus (i.e., these systems have a gentle software development
curve [40]), leading to large numbers of packages that provide
some basic functiona lity, and developers who are subject to the
sunk cost fallacy and then c ontinue to invest in their custom
packages, rather than joining forces and building community
packages. This d ivergence leads to missed opportunities for
interoperability. It is often difcult for workows to be po rted
across systems, for system components to be interchanged,
for pr ovenance to be captured and exploited in similar ways,
and for developers to leverage different execution engine s,
schedulers, or monitoring services.
A. Brief State-of-the-art and Challenges
Workow systems often grow organically: developers start
by solvin g a concrete data analysis problem and they en d up
with a new workow tool. In some cases, workow systems
may differ by design, rather than by accident. For example,
they offer fundamentally different abstractions or models for a
workow: DAG-structured vs. recursive, impe rative vs. declar-
ative, data ow v s. control (and data) ow. These fundamental
differences, catering for different use cases, make it such that
full interoperability may simply not be possible. Alternately,
workow systems have many different la yers and components
that may be inte rchangeable, e.g., workow specications, task
descriptions, data passing methods, le handling, task execu-
tion engines, e tc . Interoperability at some layers is likely to be
more impactful than others; for instance, being able to run the
same workow specicatio n (with appropriately encapsulated
task implementations) on different workow infrastructures
would be a major relief for users trying to reuse implemented
workows in other organizations. Further, interoperability does
not need to imply agr eement and for workow systems to
implement a standard interface; instead, it may occur via shim
layers or intermediate repre sentations, in a similar manner
to compiling to a high level language. With the reuse goal,
projects as eFlows4HPC p roposes the HPC Workows as a
Service (HPCWaaS) meth odology, where workows will be
dened by expert developers an d provided as a service to
community users [26].
Most efforts to unify workow systems and/or their com-
ponen ts have led to the denition of a standard developed
by a subset of the community [41], [42]. However, the spe-
cialization of some of these standards may require that other
systems conform to that specication , thus resulting in low
adoption. Attempts to standardize also may lead to overly
generic interfaces that in the end inhibit usability and lead
to hidden incom patibilities.
A particularly pressing problem at the interface of workow
technology and HPC systems is the need for a common sub-
mission model that is compatible to heterogeneous p latforms.
The differences between the ways workow engines, sched-
ulers, and execution eng ines interact is a universal challenge
faced by work ow developers when trying to ta rget multiple
infrastructures underlying long-lasting desig n decision s. Fur-
ther challenges relate to authentication and authorization mod-
els deployed on many systems (e.g., two-factor authentication).
Some efforts in this area are currently unde rgoing [43].
B. A Vision for Potential Community Activities
An immediate and continuous action would be to host
several bake-offs to compare work ow systems, including
task and workow denitions, a benchmark set of workows
with dened input data and outputs, a s well as job execution
interfaces. This would entail engaging participants to write and
execute these workows and identify ing commonalities be-
tween systems. A successful example is the GA4GH-DREAM
challenge [44]. An open question is whether such attempts
should be domain-specic or domain-overarching; there is
likely a gr eater op portunity for standardization within domains
(and indeed some domains have already made signicant
progress), but d omain-specic standards would only partly
solve the interoperability problem. The workow community
should th e n review these areas, determine and then pub liciz e
what has worked, an d build on successful prior efforts.
With the emergen ce of FaaS ( Function-as-a-Service ) sys-
tems (e.g., AWS Lamb da and Step Functions, Azure Durable
Functions, Google Cloud Functions, IBM Composer), or CaaS
(Container-as-a-Service) services (e.g. AWS Fargate, Google
Cloud Run), the community should identify a set o f sug-
gested use cases and compare them aga inst an imple mentation
with popular or recently developed FaaS-ena bled workow
systems [45][47]. Such a comparison may tu rn out com-
plementary features that can be of benet for both industry
and the workows community. In addition to features, a
set of common workow patterns could also be identied.
However, there is still some uncertainty regarding the scope of
previously developed patterns (e.g. , for representing patterns in
dynamic workows). Thus, it is necessary to survey published
patterns [48], [49] and identify gaps seen by the community.
Although the above proposed activities have the potential
to advance interoperability, the current funding and resear ch
recogn ition models of te n implicitly work against standardiza-
tion by constan tly requiring innovative ideas even in ar eas
where outreach, uptake, and maintenance rather than inno-
vation seems to be the most pressing problem. Developing
sustained funding models fo r building and evolving workow
standards, encouraging their adoption, supporting interoper-
ability, testing, and providing user and developer training
would help address these challenges.
VI. TRAINING AND EDUCATION F OR WORKFLOW USERS
There is a strong need for more, better, an d new training
and education oppo rtunities for workow users. Many users
re-invent the wheel without reusing software infrastructures
and workow tools that would make their workow execution
more convenient, more efcient, easier to evolve, and more
portable. This is partly due to the lack of comprehensive and
intuitive train ing materials that would guide users through the
process of designing a workow (besides the typical toy
examples provided in tutorials).
A. Brief State-of-the-art and Challenges
Using workow tools ca n req uire large amounts of effort
and time, due to a steep learning curve. A contributing
factor is that user s may not know the required terminology
and concepts. As a result, some have noted that wh a t would
be need ed in the cur rent technology landscape is to ship a
developer along with the workow tool.
One of the reaso ns for the above cha llenge is that there are
few recip es or cookbook s for workow systems. Further-
more, given that workows an d their execution platforms are
complex and diverse, in addition to mere training material,
there is a need for a training infrastruc ture that consists of
workows and accompanying data (small enough to be u sed
for training purposes but large enough to be meaningful) as
well as execution testbeds for running these workows.
Given the multitude of workow systems [3], and the
lack of standards (Section V), users cannot easily pick the
appropriate systems for their needs. More importantly, there is
an understandable fear of being locked into a tool that at some
point in the near future will no longer be supported. Although
docume ntation can be a problem, guidance is the mor e crucial
issue. Many users have the basic skills to create and execute
workows on some sy stem, but as requirements gr adually
increase many users evolve their simple approaches in ad-hoc
ways, thus developing/main ta ining a working but imperfect
homegrown system. There is thus a high risk of hitting tech-
nological o r labor-intensiveness roadblocks, which could be
remedied by using a workow system. But, when gradua ting
to such a system, there will likely be co nstraints that p revent
users from reproducing the functionality of the ir h omegrown
system. The benets of using the workow system should thus
largely outweigh the drawback of these constraints.
Given all the above challenges, it is not easy to reach out
to users at the appropriate time. Reach out too early and
users will no t view using a particular workow system as
compelling. Reach out too late, and users are already locked
into their homegrown system , even though in the lo ng run this
system will severely harm their produc tivity.
B. A Vision for Potential Community Activities
Lowering the entry barrier is key for en abling the next-
generation of resear c hers to benet from workow systems.
An initial app roach would be to provide a basic set of
simple, yet conceptually rich, sample workow patterns (e.g.,
hello world one-task workows, chain workows, fork-
join workows, simple dynamic workows), a ll with a few
ways of handling data and I/O, and all with a few target
execution platforms. Then workow system teams can pro-
vide (interactive) documentation (or could be hosted on a
community Web site) on how to run these patterns with their
system [37]. Additionally, mechanisms should be identied
at the institutional level to commit workow systems training
efforts in person: (i) this should be based on existing facilities
and universities efforts; (ii) the scope of the training should
be narrowed down so it is m anageable; and (iii) the issue of
who trains the trainers? needs to be addressed.
In light of workfo rce training, workow concepts should
be taug ht at early stages of the researchers/users education
path. Precisely, these concepts should be included in university
curricula, including domain scienc e curricula. Recent efforts
have produced peda gogic modules that target workow educa-
tion [50], [51]. Pedagogic content could also be distributed as
workow modules to existing software carpentry efforts [52].
There is an established community of workow researchers,
developers, and users that has extensive expertise knowledge
regarding specic tools, systems, applications, etc. It is crucial
to capture such knowledge and bootstrap a community work-
ow knowledge-base (following standards for documentation,
interoperability, etc.) for training a nd education. The work -
ows community would also benet from collaborations with
social scientists and socio logists so as to help d e ne a n overall
strategy for approaching some of the above challenges.
VII. BUILDING A WORKFLO WS COMMUNITY
Given the current large size and fragmentation of the work-
ow technology landscape, there is a clear need to establish a
cohesive community of workow developers and user s. This
community would be crucial for avoiding unnecessary duplica-
tion of e ffort and would allow for sharing, and thus growing, of
knowledge. To this end, there are four main components that
need to be addressed for building a community: (i) id e ntity
building, (ii) trust, (iii) particip ation, and (iv) rewards.
A. Brief State-of-the-art and Challenges
The most natural idea is to think of two distinct com-
munities: (i) a Workow Research and Development Com-
munity, a nd (ii) a Work ow User Community. The former
gathers people wh o share interest in workow R&D, and
correspo nding sub-disciplines. Subgroups of this community
are based on common methodologies, technical domains (e.g.,
computing, provenance, design), scientic discipline s, as well
as geographical and funding areas. The latter gathers anyone
using workows for optimization of their work processes.
However, most dom ain science users think of themselves
in their specic disciplines rst, as they just hap pen to use
workows to get their work done.
The two aforementioned c ommunities are not necessarily
disjoint, but currently have little overlap. And yet, it is crucial
that they interact. Such interactio n seems to happen only on
a case-by-case basis, rather than via organized community
efforts. One could, instead, envision a single community (e.g.,
team of users, or te am-ow) tha t gathers both workow sys-
tem developers and workow-focused users, with the c ommon
goal of spreading knowledge and adoption of workows, thus
working towards increased sharing and convergence/interop-
eration of technologies and approaches.
Establishing trust and processes is key for bringing both
communities together. There is no one-size-ts-all workow
system or solution for all domains, instead each domain
presents their own specic needs and have different preferred
ways to address problems. There is a pressing need for main-
taining documentation and dissemination that ts different
usage options and needs.
B. A Vision for Potential Community Activities
Given the above, there are a number of existing community
efforts that c ould serve as inspiration, e.g., the WorkowHub
Club [19] and Galaxy [53]. One approach is to gather experi-
ence from computing facilities where teams have successfully
adopted and are successfu lly running workow systems [37].
Another possibility is to use proposal/project reviews as mech-
anisms for spreading workow technology knowledge. Speci-
cally, nding ways to make pro posal a uthors (typically domain
scientists) aware of available te chnology would prevent their
proposed work to not entail re-inventing the wheel. Finally,
it is c lear that solving the c ommunity challenge has large
overlap with solving the edu cation challenge (Section VI).
A short-term activity would entail establishing a common
knowledge-base for workow technology so that workow
users would be able to navigate the current technology
landscape. User criteria (for navigation) need to be d e ned.
Workow system developers can add to this knowledge base
via self-reporting and could include test statuses for a set
of standard workow congurations, especially if workow
systems are deployed across sites. There is large overlap with
similar proposed community efforts identied in Sections IV
and VI.
An ambitious vision would be to establish a Workow
Guild, i.e., an organization focused on interaction and good
relationships and self-support between subscribing workow
developers and their systems, as well as dissemination of
best-practices and tools that are used in the development and
use of these systems. However, there a re still b arriers to be
conqu e red: (i) such a commu nity could be too self-reecting,
and yet still remain fragmented ; (ii) a cultural/social problem
is that creating a new system is typically more exciting for
computer scientists as opposed to re-using someones system;
and (iii) building trust and reducing internal compe tition will
be difcult, though building community identity will help the
Guild work together against external competitors.
VIII. A ROADMAP FOR WORKFLOWS RESEARCH AND
DEVELOPMENT
In the previous sections, we have listed broad challenges for
the workows community and pr oposed a vision for commu-
nity activities to address these challenges. Here, we explore
technical approach es for realizing (part of) that vision. Based
on the outcomes of the rst summit [7], we identied three
technical thrusts for discussion in the second sum mit [8]. Some
of these thrusts align with a single theme of the rst sum mit
and some are cross-cutting. In the following subsection s, we
present the summary of discussions at the second summit
and propose r oadmap mileston es that emerged from these
discussions. Additional details can be found in [8], and a
summary of the roadmap milestones is shown in Table II.
A. Dening Common Workow Patterns and Benchmarks
The above sections point to strong needs for establishing
repositories of common workow patterns and benchmarks
(Sections III, IV, V, and VI). One objective is to develop
workow patter ns in which each pattern should b e easy for
users to leverage a s starting point for their own specic work-
ow applications they should provide links to one or more
implementations, where each implementation is for a particular
workow system and can be downloaded and easily modied
by the user. However, the level of abstraction of these patterns
(i.e., the level of connection to real application use-ca ses)
should still be dened. At one extreme, workow patterns
could b e completely abstract with no connection to any rea l-
world application. At the other extreme, workow p atterns
could b e completely use-case-driven and correspond to a ctual
scientic applications, with realistic task computations and
data sets. The end goal is thus to identify useful patterns that
span the spectrum of possible levels of abstraction.
Another aspect is the level of detail with which a pattern
species the platform on which it is to be executed and
the logistics of the execution on that platform . The platform
description could be left completely abstract, or it could
be fully specied. Und er-specifying execution platforms and
logistics may rende r the patter n not useful, but over-specifying
them could render the pattern too niche.
Benchmark specications should make it easy for workow
system developers to develop th e m or to determine that their
system cannot implement these specications. Each bench-
mark should provide links to implementations and data sets,
where each im plementation is for a particular workow sys-
tem. These implementations would be provided, maintaine d,
and evolved by workow system developers. They sho uld
be able to be packaged so that they are executed out of
the box on th e classes of p latforms they support. Moreover,
the input data o f these workows should be congurable in
size to enable both weak and strong scaling experiments. For
all congurations, also the output of the workow must be
provided to allow functio nal testing.
Given the above, the following mileston es are proposed:
M1. D ene small sets (betwee n 5 and 10) of workow patterns
and of workow benchmark deliverables. These should be
dened by eliciting feedback from users and workow system
developers, as well a s based on existing sources that provide
or dene real-world or synthetic workow pattern s.
M2. Work with a selected set of workow systems to imple-
ment the above patterns and benchmarks.
M3. Investigate options for automatic generation of patterns
and/or benchm arks using existing approaches [39], [54].
M4. Identify or create a centralized repository to host and
curate the above patterns and benchmarks [19], [39].
B. Paths Toward Interoperability of Workow Systems
Workow systems differ at varying degrees such as expres-
sivity, execution models, and ecosystems. These differences
are ma inly d ue to individual implementations of language,
control mechanisms (e.g ., fault tolerance, loops), data manage-
ment mechanisms, execution backends, reproducibility asp e cts
for sharing workows, and provenance and FAIR metadata
capturing. The need for interoperability is paramount and
can happen at multiple technical levels (e.g., task, tools,
workows, d ata, m etadata, provenance, and packaging) as well
as non-technical level including semantics, organizatio nal, and
legal issues (e.g., licenses compatibility, data sharing policies).
The need for interoperability of workow applications and
systems is c ommonly mo deled as a problem of porting ap-
plications across systems, which may require days up to
weeks of development effort [55], [56]. Most of the p revious
approa c hes for tacklin g th e interoperability problem attempted
to develop complete vertical solutions. However, there is no
attempt to develop an approach from a perspective of making
interoperable components. Interoperable components require
standardized APIs, which are still an open challenge [57], [58].
There is a tendency to tie the workow with its execution
model and data structures (e.g, the intertwine between the
abstract work ow and its execution). Understanding which
component in the workow system architecture accounts for
which functionality, is then par a mount. Thus, separation of
concerns is key for interoperability at m a ny levels, e.g. separa -
tion of orchestration of the workow graph from its execution.
Given the above, the following milestones are pr oposed:
M5. Dene concrete notions o f interoperability for different
stakeholders, in particular workow designers, workow sys-
tem designer, and workow execution organiza tions.
M6. Establish a requirements document per a small set
of abstraction layers that will (i) capture the commona lities
between components of workow systems; and (ii) perform a
separation of concern s to identify interope rability gaps.
M7. Develop real-world workow benchma rks featuring d if-
ferent congurations and complexities (see previous section).
Such benchmarks would be key to evaluate the functionality
of workow systems and compu ting platf orms systematically.
M8. Develop use cases for interoperability based on real-life
scenarios, e.g., porting workows acro ss platforms that would
provide different le system and/or different resour c e manager.
M9. Develop common API s that repr esent a set of workow
library c omponents, so a s interoperability could be ach ieved
at the comp onent level [1 7], [59], [60], including APIs for
dening inputs, storing intermediate results, and output data.
M10. Establish a workow systems developer community. An
immediate activity would be to d evelop a centralized repos-
itory of workow-related research papers, and a workow
system registry aimed at DevOps and/or users.
C. Improving Workow Systems Interface with Legacy and
Emerging HPC Software and Hardware Stacks
Improving the interface between workow systems and
existing as well as emerging HPC and cloud stacks is p a r-
ticularly important as workows are designed to be used for
long perio ds of time and may be moved between computing
providers. This challenge is exacerbated with the specialization
of ha rdware and software systems (e.g., with accelerators,
virtualization, containers, and cloud or serverless infrastruc-
tures). Thus, it is important to address the challenges faced by
workow systems with respect to discovering and interacting
with a diverse set of cyberinfrastructu re resources and also the
difculties a uthenticating remote connections while adhering
to facility policies.
Workow systems require a standard method for querying
a site on how to use that site, for example, inf ormation
about the batch system, le system conguration, data transfer
methods, and machine capa bilities. It is cruc ia l then to r st
understand what in formation is needed by workow systems,
what info rmation could be made available programmatically
and what would need to be manually cur ated (similar ongoing
efforts [61] may provide the foundations for this effort).
A key capability provided by workow systems is remote
job execution, which is necessary in cases where workows
span facilities. However, authentication has always been chal-
lenging. Many workow systems rely on fragile SSH con-
nections and in th e past the use of GSISSH for delegated
authenticatio n. Recently, sites have moved towards two factor
authenticatio n and even OAuth-based solutions. There are
though ongoin g efforts to provide programmatic identity and
access management in scientic domains [62], [63]. While the
topic of remote authentica tion is much more broad than the
workows community, the re are important considerations that
should b e included in this discussion related to progr ammatic
access, community credentials, and long- term access.
Given the above, the following mileston es are proposed:
M11. Document a machine-readable de scription of the essen-
tial properties of popular sites, e.g., dene a JSON schema
and share it on GitHub.
M12. Document remote authentication requirements fro m the
workow perspective and organize an event involving work-
ow system developers, end users, authentica tion technology
providers, and facility op e rators.
IX. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have documented and summarized the
wealth of information acquired as a result of a series of virtual
events entitled the Workows Community Summit. The goal
of these summits was to identify the common and current
challenges faced by the workows community, and outline a
vision for short- and long-term com munity activities. From this
vision, we have dened a community roadmap consisting of 12
milestones, which proposes solutions and technical approaches
for achieving that vision. This initial series of successful events
bespoke the need for continued engagement among workow
researchers, developers, and users, as well as e nlarging the
scope of the community to also embrace key stakeholders (e.g.,
computing facility operators, funding agency representatives,
etc.) for enabling the proposed vision and roadmap.
